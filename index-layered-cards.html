FAQ

Common Questions

What age does TAP Athletes work with?

We work with pitchers ages 8 and up. Each program targets a different stage:

  • Foundations — Ages 8+, all skill levels. Each pitcher starts with an in-person assessment, gets placed at the right phase, and advances by demonstrating execution.
  • Pitching Accelerator — 14U playing level and higher, for athletes at the inflection point.
  • Train Unprompted — Ages 13+ (Junior High and up), for athletes who need structure between sessions.

The Accelerator and Train Unprompted both expect some pitching experience already.

Do you offer private instruction outside of programs?

Yes. Outside of our structured programs we offer:

  • Individual instruction
  • Live at-bats
  • Rapsodo scripted bullpens
  • Standalone classes — Pitch Design, Beyond the Pitch, and rotating topics

All booked inside the TAP Athletes app. No long-term commitment required.

What makes TAP Athletes different from a private pitching coach?

The difference isn't the technology or the facility — it's how, what, and why we teach pitching.

Pitching-only focus

No hitting, no fielding, no general baseball. We go deeper into pitching, not wider — and we offer private instruction, classes, and cohort programs all under one roof.

Full-time pitching instructors

No other job, no other career. Pitcher development is the whole job.

A documented curriculum

Defined progression and regression, with real end points to every phase. Not session-by-session improvisation.

Independent training options

Beyond coached sessions, athletes can train under our umbrella without a coach instructing every rep. Live at-bats, Rapsodo scripted bullpens, and bookable independent training slots let pitchers grow safely — with our facility, our drill plans, and our standards in place.

Finish lines

Every program has one — Foundations milestones, the 60/10 Club, and the Accelerator's velocity + command guarantee.

Why introduced, not standard:

Junior high athletes are entering puberty and beginning structured strength work at school — many are already lifting in football or PE. We mirror that progression carefully. External load is introduced under direct supervision, scaled to the individual, and paired with mechanical work.

Why warmups, not resistance:

J-Bands are part of every junior high athlete's pre-throw routine — activating the rotator cuff and shoulder stabilizers before any throwing begins. They're not used for resistance training at this stage. The body's structural maturity drives that decision: warmup activation is appropriate, but heavy resisted band work isn't.

Why introduced at this stage:

Rapsodo enters during junior high in tracked bullpens, not on every session. Athletes start seeing their own pitch metrics — velocity, spin, movement — but data isn't yet the focus of training. The goal at this stage is exposure and awareness. By high school, the data becomes a regular part of how training decisions get made.

What "effort bands" means:

Younger athletes don't train to a velocity number. Instead, throws are organized into effort bands — for example, 50% / 70% / 100% — to teach intent and self-regulation. The radar reads the actual output, helping the athlete and coach understand whether the body is matching the intent. It's a teaching tool, not a goal-setting one.

Why light long-toss only:

Middle-school athletes begin building arm strength through controlled, distance-based long-toss — emphasizing clean throwing patterns and effortless distance, not maximum-effort pulldowns. The progression to true pulldowns happens in junior high, when the body can handle the volume and intensity safely.

What "monitored independence" looks like:

Junior high athletes start running through drill blocks without a coach talking on every rep. The coach is in the room, observing — but stepping back. Athletes are allowed to make mistakes and self-correct, which is how real ownership begins. Full independent training capacity comes in high school. Until then, the coach stays close enough to step in when it matters.
